Some Explantions!
  Doppler Effect: This is a demonstration of the doppler effect, where the wavelengths of a wave of a specific frequency change depending on its movement towards or away from an observer. In the demo, there is a green point that is moving up and down and generating a wave that travels away from it while doing so. When the dot starts moving back and forth, the wave gets squished and stretched by the movement, demonstrating the doppler effect.
  Wave Interfernece 1: This is a demonstration of how waves can be added and subtracted from each other when they interfere. In the demo, you can create a complex wave by adding together numerous other waves that you can control the wavelength, magnitude, and phase-shift of to see how they interfere with each other.
  Wave interference 2: This is a demonstration of waves interfering with each other across a plane. In the demo, there are two points that generate waves whose wavelength, magnitude, and phase-shift can be controlled, and that radiate out from the points. The interfference between the two waves can be seen across a 2d plane as they radiate outwards away from their origin.
  Wave interference 3: This is essentially the same as wave interference 2, but seen in a 3d viewport to give a better sense of the interference of the waves.
  Mirrors 1: This is a demonstration of how light reflects off of curved mirrors and forms images. In the demo, an objects locaion and height can be controlled, and a ray diagram is shown of how it would theoretically reflect off of a spherical (though since it is in 2d, it would be circular) mirror with a controlled focal point. This demo uses a more ideal approach to this, where all incident light rays reflect exactly at the line tangent to the mirrors surface where it meets the principal axis, thus making (1/s_i) + (1/s_o) = (1/f) hold true as it is taught in AP Physics 2.
  Mirrors 2: This is a similar demonstration of how light reflects off of a shperical/circular mirror, but with each light ray reflecting at the point where it actually reaches the mirror.
